Uncle Sam also fights the mad scientist Dr. Dirge, the King Killer, and the insanity-causing Mad Poet".<ref>{{cite book |last1=Nevins |first1=Jess |title=Encyclopedia of Golden Age Superheroes |date=2013 |publisher=High Rock Press |isbn=978-1-61318-023-5 |page=281}}</ref>\n\n===DC Comics===\n[[DC Comics]] acquired the character as part of its acquisition of the Quality characters in the 1950s, and he was used as a supporting character in \'\'[[Justice League|Justice League of America]]\'\' in the 1970s. This established Uncle Sam as the leader of the [[Freedom Fighters (comics)|Freedom Fighters]], a team of former Quality characters that briefly received its own title.<ref name="dc-free">{{Cite book | last = Greenberger | first = Robert | author-link = Robert Greenberger | contribution = Freedom Fighters | editor-last = Dougall | editor-first = Alastair | title = The DC Comics Encyclopedia | pages = 131 | publisher = [[Dorling Kindersley]] | place = New York  | year = 2008 | isbn = 978-0-7566-4119-1 | oclc = 213309017 }}</ref> This team was initially based on a parallel world called [[Earth-X]], where [[World War II]] had lasted into the 1970s.\n\nUncle Sam\'s origin was [[retroactive continuity|rewritten]] somewhat in \'\'[[Spectre (comics)|The Spectre]]\'\',<ref>\'\'The Spectre\'\' (vol. 3) #37–38</ref> where Uncle Sam is described as a spiritual entity created through an occult ritual by the [[Founding Fathers of the United States|Founding Fathers]]. This "Spirit of America" was initially bound to a powerful talisman and would take physical form by merging with a dying patriot. The new origin states that the Spirit of America had taken human form as the [[Minute-Man]] during the Revolutionary War, [[Brother Jonathan]] in later conflicts and, during the [[American Civil War]], had been split in two as [[Johnny Rebel|Johnny Reb]] and [[Billy Yank]].\n\nThe Spirit first assumed its now-familiar Uncle Sam incarnation in 1870, when it resurrected a political [[cartoonist]] who had been killed by [[William M. Tweed|Boss Tweed]]. The second host of Uncle Sam fought in [[World War I]]. A third (the character\'s Golden Age incarnation) was a superhero during World War II but vanished at the end of the war, erasing any subsequent appearances from the fictional history of the [[DC Universe]] (although most of them had already been erased by the \'\'[[Crisis on Infinite Earths]]\'\'). In \'\'The Spectre\'\', the Spirit is resurrected in a new costumed form called the \'\'\'Patriot\'\'\', but later reverts to Uncle Sam in a \'\'[[Superman]]\'\' issue.{{issue|date=August 2011}}\n\nA 1997 Vertigo series features the character with the persona of a street person.<ref>{{cite book |title=The Dark Age: Grim, Great & Gimmicky Post-Modern Comics| last= Voger| first= Mark| year= 2006| publisher= TwoMorrows Publishing| isbn= 978-1-893905-53-5|page=115}}</ref> A similar notion was suggested by [[Alan Moore]] in his 1980s crossover proposal \'\'[[Twilight of the Superheroes]]\'\'; this interpretation was inspired by the satirical novel \'\'[[The Public Burning]]\'\' by [[Robert Coover]], which also features a superheroic version of Uncle Sam.\n\nIn \'\'[[Infinite Crisis]]\'\' #1, the Freedom Fighters are attacked by the [[Secret Society of Super Villains]]. Three of the Freedom Fighters, [[Human Bomb#Roy Lincoln|Human Bomb]], [[Phantom Lady#Dee Tyler|Phantom Lady]], and [[Black Condor#Ryan Kendall|Black Condor]] are killed in the battle. Uncle Sam himself seemingly dies at the hands of [[Sinestro]]. The other team members are brutally injured but survive. Uncle Sam is seen face down in rainwater.\nWhen the dead heroes are found strung up on the [[Washington Monument]] in #2, Uncle Sam is missing.\n\nThe character\'s latest incarnation appeared in the first issue of \'\'[[Freedom Fighters (comics)#Uncle Sam and the Freedom Fighters|Uncle Sam and the Freedom Fighters]]\'\', and spends the first few issues of this new series attempting to form a new version of the Freedom Fighters. This new Uncle Sam emerges from the [[Mississippi River]] at the same time as [[Father Time (DC Comics)|Father Time]] is elsewhere planning the future of [[S.H.A.D.E.]] with new incarnations of the Freedom Fighters members. Uncle Sam, disturbed by the deadly force used by the new versions of [[Phantom Lady#Stormy Knight|Phantom Lady]], [[Human Bomb#Andy Franklin|The Human Bomb]], [[Doll Man#Lester Colt|Doll Man]], and others, successfully recruits these [[metahuman]]s into his new Freedom Fighters team, which results in Father Time ordering his remaining S.H.A.D.E. personnel to pursue and kill Uncle Sam and his team. Although Uncle Sam is shown to be against killing, particularly rebuking Doll Man for murdering a crime lord in front of the man\'s young grandson (in issue #1), Uncle Sam is not against using deadly force when necessary.<ref>\'\'Uncle Sam and the Freedom Fighters\'\' #1–8 (2006)</ref>\n\nHe is also seen in \'\'Final Crisis\'\' #4 and classified as Corrupted when [[Alan Scott]] is looking for remaining superhumans to fight Darkseid\'s forces.<ref>\'\'Final Crisis\'\' #4 (2008)</ref>\n\nUncle Sam shows up in the [[Blackest Night]] crossover, helping many other superheroes fight the returning dead. The [[Pre-Crisis]] version of Uncle Sam could at one point travel to parallel universes, as he did with the Freedom Fighters to travel to Earth-X from [[Earth-Two]],<ref>\'\'[[All-Star Squadron]]\'\' #36</ref> but in another instance, Uncle Sam and the other Freedom Fighters require the help of a scientist\'s machine to travel to [[Earth-One]].<ref>\'\'Freedom Fighters\'\' #1</ref> His powers are said to be in direct proportion of the belief people have in the idea of the United States as The City Upon a Hill. In \'\'Uncle Sam and the Freedom Fighters\'\', Father Time states that tests indicate Sam is not a human, metahuman, or magical being, and the indication of what he \'\'is\'\' can be considered inconclusive.
