[[fictional]] characters published by [[DC Comics]]. The first three are [[African-American]] [[superheroes]] and are members of the same family. The first Amazing-Man debuted in \'\'[[All-Star Squadron]]\'\' #23 (July 1983), and was created by [[Roy Thomas]] and [[Jerry Ordway]]. The second Amazing-Man debuted in \'\'[[Justice League|Justice League America]]\'\' #86 (March 1994), and was created by Dan Vado and Marc Campos. The third Amazing-Man debuted in \'\'[[Justice Society of America]]\'\' vol. 3 #12 (March 2008), and was created by [[Geoff Johns]] and [[Dale Eaglesham]]. The fourth Amazing Man debuted in \'\'[[OMAC (comics)|OMAC]]\'\' vol. 3 #2 (December 2011), and was created by [[Dan DiDio]] and [[Keith Giffen]].\n\n==Publication history==\nAlthough a 1980s creation of writer [[Roy Thomas]], the Amazing-Man published by [[DC Comics]] was placed in the 1940s and made a contemporary of various [[Golden Age of comic books|Golden Age]] superheroes. The character was created by Roy Thomas as a tribute to [[Bill Everett]]\'s [[Amazing-Man (Centaur Publications)|Amazing-Man]], a character he created for [[Centaur Publications]] during the so-called [[Golden Age of Comic Books]].<ref>{{Google books|aXqPtKhc4IMC|The All-Star Companion|page=76|keywords=amazing+man|text=|plainurl=}}</REF>\n\n==Fictional character biography==\n===Will Everett===\nWill Everett was a promising young [[African-American]] [[Olympic Games|Olympian]] who had competed in the [[1936 Summer Olympics]] in [[Berlin]], but his post-Olympic career devolved into a janitorial profession at a laboratory owned by [[Cyclotron (comics)|Dr. Terry Curtis]]. During an accident involving the explosion of some equipment to which he was exposed (developed by the criminal mastermind the [[Ultra-Humanite]]), Everett quickly developed the ability to mimic whatever properties he touched (similar to [[Marvel Comics]]\' [[Absorbing Man]]). For example, if he touched [[steel]], then his body became composed of steel.<ref name="dc-ency">{{Cite book | last = Jimenez | first = Phil | author-link = Phil Jimenez | contribution = Amazing Man I | editor-last = Dougall | editor-first = Alastair | title = The DC Comics Encyclopedia | pages = 12 | publisher = [[Dorling Kindersley]] | place = London | year = 2008 | isbn = 978-0-7566-4119-1}}</ref>\n\n====All-Star Squadron====\n{{main|All-Star Squadron}}\nAt first, he was employed by the Ultra-Humanite as a henchman along with Curtis (as Cyclotron) and [[Deathbolt]].<ref>{{Cite comic|Title = All-Star Squadron|Issue = #23|Date = July 1983|Publisher = [[DC Comics]]}}</ref> However, his sympathies soon swayed towards the side of good<ref>{{Cite comic|Title = All-Star Squadron|Issue = #25|Date = September 1983|Publisher = DC Comics}}</ref> after repeated exposure to the [[All-Star Squadron]], a team of both Golden Age characters and retroactive characters like himself, with whom he joined to defeat his former employer\'s machinations. He then served a lengthy stint as a member of this voluminous mystery man organization.\n\nIn February 1942, the Squadron helped Everett defeat the bigoted villain in his home town of [[Detroit]], the Real American.<ref>{{Cite comic|Title = All-Star Squadron|Issue = #38|Date = October 1984|Publisher = DC Comics}}</ref> During the first [[Crisis on Infinite Earths|Crisis]], Amazing-Man was one of a group of heroes chosen by the Monitor to stop the Anti-Monitor\'s quest for destruction.<ref>{{Cite comic|Title = All-Star Squadron|Issue = #53|Date = January 1986|Publisher = DC Comics}}</ref><ref>{{Cite comic|Title = Crisis on Infinite Earths|Issue = #5|Date = August 1985|Publisher = DC Comics}}</ref> On a future case, Amazing-Man\'s powers changed so that now he had mastery of [[magnetism]] while losing his ability to mimic matter.\n\n====Civil Rights Activist====\nIn the 1950s, his secret identity was revealed to the general public by [[J. Edgar Hoover]]. This act endangered the lives of Everett\'s wife and family. During the [[Civil Rights Movement]] of the 1960s, the murder of his nephew alongside two other civil rights activists spurred his involvement in the Civil Rights Movement of the time. He led marches against segregation across the United States, and also helped to quell riots in [[Detroit]]. Everett was also responsible for the capture of [[Martin Luther King Jr.]]\'s murderer, [[James Earl Ray]]. In the DC Comics Universe, he is considered the third most important advocate for [[African American]] civil rights, behind only King and [[Malcolm X]].<ref name=jsa12>\'\'Justice Society of America\'\' vol. 3 #12 (March 2008)</ref>\n\n====Heirs====\nIt was later revealed that his grandson, Will Everett III (a.k.a. "Junior") also developed mimicry abilities. Will Everett Senior was last seen in the hospital, visited by his grandson Will Everett III. The senior Everett was dying of cancer.<ref>{{Cite comic|Title = Justice League America|Issue = #86-87|Date = March - April 1994|Publisher = DC Comics}}, \'\'DC Comics Encyclopedia\'\'</ref> The status of his son, the father of Amazing-Man III, is currently unknown. For a brief time, his grandson carried on the legacy of Amazing-Man before dying tragically. Later, another grandson named Markus Clay would take up the mantle of Amazing-Man.\n\n===Will Everett III===\n[[Image:ExtremeJustice 16.jpg|150px|thumb|left|Will Everett III and [[Maxima Bonn is defeated by Kho and subsequently absorbed by [[Brother Eye]], who states that he may prove useful at a later date.<ref>\'\'OMAC\'\' #2</ref>\n\n==Powers and abilities==\n* Will Everett was originally capable of transforming himself into a living, breathing facsimile of any material that he touched. Later Will Everett\'s powers were altered, and he was instead able to magnetically attract or repel objects with his hands.\n* Will Everett III could cause his body to duplicate the properties of any inorganic material he touched from stone to glass. If he touched the pavement, for example, he became a sentient being made of living stone, with all its commensurate strengths and weaknesses. Will Everett III could also absorb and duplicate vast amounts of energy, as when he defeated the Overmaster by draining and duplicating its powers.\n* Markus Clay appears to possess the same abilities as the first two Amazing-Men.\n\n==In other media==\nIn \'\'[[The Flash (2014 TV series)|The Flash]]\'\' episode "Power Outage", Will Everett is mentioned as one of the people who seemingly died as a result of the particle accelerator accident that gave The Flash his powers.\n\nIn season 4 of \'\'[[Young Justice: Phantoms|Young Justice]]\'\', Will Everett as the Amazing-Man is mentioned by the assassin [[List of DC Comics characters: O|Onyx]] as her superhero maternal grandfather.
